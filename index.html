Given all the recent headlines and market reports, we are clearly in a buyer's market as we approach year-end. Prospective buyers continue to exercise caution due to numerous factors, including the negative impact of the 2018 SALT deduction cap, this year's revised mansion tax, and the recently passed New York City rent regulations. These government-imposed policy changes, coupled with an oversupply of luxury new development condos, the retreat of foreign buyers, a strong dollar, uncertainty regarding the trade wars, the chaotic political climate in Washington, and the upcoming election year have all added to a mood of uncertainty and a cooling market.
 
On the other hand, the local NYC economy is healthy with a growing and vibrant technology sector. The stock market is reaching record highs, and mortgage rates remain historically low. Although the city's residential market has been in a downward trend since 2015, as evidenced by rising for sale inventory, the bid-ask spread is finally starting to narrow. Across all price segments and submarkets, owners have become more realistic regarding selling their homes and are now behaving more strategically regarding their asking price. In many cases where buyers see value and opportunity, deals are closing. We see this trend continuing as we move forward into the New Year, and 2020 will likely be an excellent opportunity for buyers!
​
Buyers can be quite successful in this type of market climate, especially with some smart planning.
 
  • Before initiating the search process, if you plan to finance, obtain a mortgage pre-approval. Knowing what you can afford will make it easier to focus on your price parameters and bolster your negotiating power.
  • Complete the REBNY Financial Statement in advance. Your financial summary, along with the mortgage pre-approval, will provide the seller with your overall financial picture, helping them evaluate you as a buyer candidate and determine whether you are likely to receive board approval. Sample REBNY Financial Statement templates can be found online or obtained from your real estate agent.
    • Ideally, co-op boards like to see two years of post-closing liquidity (mortgage and maintenance payments), a stable employment history and a debt-to-income ratio not to exceed 30 percent. The same is true for all-cash buyers.
  • I also always encourage my clients to engage an attorney with NYC co-op and condo expertise early in the process. This helps buyers address any legal questions along the way and sets them up to move forward once an offer is accepted and parties proceed to the contract phase.
  • To determine the ideal residence within your budget, explore different neighborhoods and attend open houses — they're an easy way to preview several apartments in a defined time frame.
    • In a buyer's market, individual submarkets and price points behave differently and the supply/demand dynamics are not the same. This is all the more reason to do your homework and to truly understand the neighborhood where you are focusing your search. For example, the West Village is not the same as the Upper East Side.
    • Narrowing your search to your desired neighborhood will streamline the process and make you more focused on available inventory to meet your criteria. 
    • Open houses help you become an educated buyer by gauging, in person, overall market activity and buyer interest in specific properties.
  • To understand a property's value and determine your offer, you must understand the amount of available inventory and review a survey of comparable properties in your target submarket.
    • A qualified agent will help you examine all active, in-contract and sold listings in each selected submarket. Determining whether you have 50 one-bedroom apartments to choose from or only 20 will have a significant impact on how you proceed.
    • To gauge the overall pulse of the market, know that in-contract listings are most valuable in terms of real-time data. (Sale prices at contract aren't recorded, so your broker will have to use peer relationships to gather this inside information).
    • Sold or closed listings are relevant, but they likely went into contract up to 90 days prior. The market is never static.
  • Know that a listing's days on market (DOM) will have a major impact on your negotiability. You are likely to receive a more substantial price discount for a property that has been on the market for 120 days versus 30 days.
  • Determine your offer in the context of the current market, your strength as a buyer and the condition of the property. Since we are in a buyer's market, your strategy might include making an offer under market value once you assess how long the property has been on the market and its overall condition.
    • If a property needs a gut renovation, of course, that needs to be considered in your bid. On the other hand, you want to be reasonable and not insult the seller.
    • That being said, DOM is a telling metric and will guide your offer strategy.
